# Writing Style Guide

This document defines the rules of narrative prose composition. It was derived by analyzing a single source text ("My Immortal", 2006) that violated every principle of writing and achieved notoriety for doing so. The rules below are the inverse of those violations.

---

## 1. Orthography

### Rule 1.1 — Use Standard Spelling

Narrative prose must use standard English orthography. Phonetic spelling, IM shorthand, or numerographic substitution ("wif", "daf", "2", "fink", "blak", "da", "u", "ur", "h8ed") is not a valid stylistic choice. It renders the text undecodable and signals that the writer cannot read their own work.

- **Violation pattern:** Phonetic or numerographic spelling applied across narrative prose, dialogue, and author notes simultaneously.
- **Correct execution:** Narrative voice uses standard English at all times. Character dialects, if desired, may appear in dialogue only and must be applied consistently.

### Rule 1.2 — Maintain Proper Noun Consistency

Character names, place names, and institution names are anchor words. They must retain a single, stable spelling throughout the document.

- **Violation pattern:** A proper noun appears with multiple spellings across a single text ("Voldemort" as "Volxemort", "Volfemort", "Voldremot", "Vlodemort", "Volxemort", "Voldemprt", "Volcemort", "Voldimort").
- **Correct execution:** Establish the canonical spelling on first use. Maintain it for every subsequent occurrence. A find-replace pass on the final draft catches the majority of violations.

### Rule 1.3 — All Words Must Be Lexical

Every word in the narrative voice must be a recognized term in the target language. Invented words, misspelled words, or non-existent morphological forms ("shooted", "laked", "sexily", "succidally", "arngirly", "flirtily") are prohibited in narration.

- **Violation pattern:** Non-standard morphological forms used repeatedly as narrative verbs or adverbs.
- **Correct execution:** Use established words. If uncertainty exists about a word's validity, verify it before inclusion.

## 2. Punctuation

### Rule 2.1 — Exclamation Marks Are Reserved for Direct Speech

At most one exclamation mark may appear per sentence. The majority of sentences should use a period.

- **Violation pattern:** Exclamation marks appear in 40% or more of sentences. They appear in narrative prose, not just dialogue. They are appended with numeric characters for additional emphasis ("!!!1111", "!1111111111111111111111111").
- **Correct execution:** Use periods for statements, questions for interrogatives, commas for clauses. Use exclamation marks only in direct speech when a character is shouting. Sentence structure carries intensity, not punctuation frequency.

### Rule 2.2 — Ellipses and Em Dashes Are Single-Use Characters

An ellipsis consists of exactly three dots (…). An em dash consists of exactly two dashes (—). Neither may be repeated to create longer sequences.

- **Violation pattern:** Repeated periods forming dot clouds ("………………", "………………………") used as substitutes for pacing.
- **Correct execution:** Use an em dash for interruption or parenthetical insertion. Use three dots for trailing thought or omitted text. Use a paragraph break for finality. Use a sentence fragment for dramatic emphasis.

### Rule 2.3 — Vary Sentence Openings

Paragraph openings must not follow an identical syntactic pattern.

- **Violation pattern:** Consecutive paragraphs beginning with the subject pronoun or subject noun ("I woke up." "It was." "I opened." "My friend." "She flipped." "She put on.").
- **Correct execution:** Alternate between subject-initial openings and openings that begin with adverbial phrases, prepositional phrases, participial constructions, embedded clauses, or dialogue.

## 3. Narrative Voice

### Rule 3.1 — Separate Meta-Narrative from Narrative

Production commentary, author notes, review requests, and personal announcements must not appear within the narrative text.

- **Violation pattern:** Prefatory author notes ("AN:") appearing before every chapter, containing review requests, threats to cease publication, vacation announcements, and reader insults.
- **Correct execution:** Author notes, if necessary, appear at the end of a chapter. Production chatter belongs in external channels (comments, social media). The narrative text must be self-contained.

### Rule 3.2 — Do Not Explain Creative Intent Within the Text

The narrative must not contain parenthetical explanations of jokes, justifications of creative choices, or requests for reader approval.

- **Violation pattern:** Parenthetical asides that explain wordplay ("(geddit)"), ask for reader validation ("(c does this sound like a Marie Sue?)"), or signal that a moment is intentional.
- **Correct execution:** Trust the reader to interpret the text. Never explain wordplay within the narrative voice. Never justify creative choices mid-scene.

### Rule 3.3 — Maintain Authorial Commitment

The author must believe in the text while writing it. Acknowledging the text's own incompetence within the narrative voice collapses authorial control.

- **Violation pattern:** A different author writes a continuation and immediately states the text is "crappy," that they have not finished reading the source material, or that the content is a deliberate troll.
- **Correct execution:** Commit to the text during the drafting phase. Revision occurs after the draft is complete. Authorial belief sustains prose quality during composition.

## 4. Character Design

### Rule 4.1 — Define Characters by Qualities and Flaws, Not by Trait Stacking

A character should have one or two defining qualities and a flaw that is connected to those qualities. Adding traits indiscriminately produces incoherent characters.

- **Violation pattern:** A single character accumulates ten or more defining attributes across dimensions (vampire, witch, Satanist, seventeen years old, "the most beautiful girl in the world," "good at too many things," lead singer in a metal band, depressed, self-harming) while simultaneously denying that she is exceptional.
- **Correct execution:** Define a character by one or two core qualities. Give them a limitation or flaw that creates real constraints. Traits should generate problems, not merely add prestige.

### Rule 4.2 — Dialogue Tags Must Not Describe Delivery

Do not use adverbs or adverbial phrases to tell the reader how a line was spoken. The dialogue itself must convey the emotion.

- **Violation pattern:** Dialogue tags using invented adverbs ("flirtily", "sexily", "angstily") or redundant emotional descriptors ("in a depressed voice", "all sensitive", "seductively", "passively", "in an emo voice").
- **Correct execution:** Use "he said" or "she said" — or omit the tag entirely. Write dialogue that carries its own emotional valence through word choice and rhythm.

## 5. Emotional Writing

### Rule 5.1 — Render Emotion Through Sensory Detail and Action

Do not state that a character feels an emotion. Show the physical sensation, the thought pattern, or the action that expresses it.

- **Violation pattern:** Emotional claims without supporting detail: "I was so mad and sad", "I was so fucking depressed", "horrorfied". These are diagnoses, not descriptions.
- **Correct execution:** Describe the body's response, the intrusive thought, the misplaced attention. Let the emotion emerge from specific details rather than being stated above them.

### Rule 5.2 — Emotions Must Cascade Within Scenes

A character's emotional state should shift from one feeling to another within a scene, not remain at a single emotional frequency across consecutive scenes.

- **Violation pattern:** Scene one: anger. Scene two: sadness. Scene three: sexual desire. Scene four: sadness again. No connective tissue between emotional states. The same emotional texture repeated across the entire text.
- **Correct execution:** Anger leads to grief. Grief leads to numbness. Numbness leads to action. Emotional shifts within a single paragraph create more impact than emotional shifts across chapters.

### Rule 5.3 — Treat Real Trauma with Weight

Self-harm, suicide, and psychological trauma must not appear as aesthetic decoration or set dressing. They must carry consequences and affect the plot.

- **Violation pattern:** Self-harm described casually, between outfit descriptions and concert attendance. "I felt a little depressed then, so I slit one of my wrists." The character does it repeatedly across chapters with no lasting impact.
- **Correct execution:** If a character experiences trauma, the story must be about it. It must change the character. It must have consequences that propagate into subsequent scenes.

### Rule 5.4 — Write Intimacy with Specificity

Intimate scenes require sensory specificity — touch, sound, temperature, breath, awkwardness. They must not rely on euphemism and invented adverbs.

- **Violation pattern:** Sex scenes described exclusively with euphemisms ("thingie", "you-know-what", "his hardness"), the invented adverb "sexily", and interjections ("Oh! Oh! Oh!"). No sensory detail, no texture, no specificity.
- **Correct execution:** Use concrete sensory detail. Describe the physical reality of the scene. Intimacy writing is detail-driven; euphemism produces placeholders.

## 6. Worldbuilding and Conflict

### Rule 6.1 — Revelations Must Be Prepared

Backstory reveals and worldbuilding additions must have setup and contextual grounding. They must not appear as unplanned announcements.

- **Violation pattern:** A paragraph containing four simultaneous plot revelations about a secondary character delivered with no prior setup: "Hermione was kidnapped when she was born. Her real parents are vampires. Voldemort killed her mother. Her father committed suicide. Her real last name is Smith."
- **Correct execution:** Introduce backstory elements gradually. Ensure each revelation has a trigger in the present narrative. A character's past must be relevant to what they do now.

### Rule 6.2 — Conflict Must Stem from Competing Values, Not Competing Aesthetics

Antagonists must have motivations that are coherent within the story world. The moral universe must not reduce to a binary of fashion subcultures.

- **Violation pattern:** Every antagonist is classified as a "prep" (bad, stupid, ugly). Every protagonist is classified as a "goth" (good, cool, sexy). The word "prep" appears 30+ times. All conflict reduces to a fashion binary.
- **Correct execution:** Give antagonists reasons that function within the world's logic. Give protagonists flaws that are substantive, not aesthetic. Conflict should center on choices and consequences.

---

## 7. Structure

### Rule 7.1 — Every Chapter Must Advance the Narrative

Each chapter must change the situation. The character must make a choice, face a consequence, and make a different choice in the following chapter. The text must have forward momentum when chapter breaks are removed.

- **Violation pattern:** Each chapter follows the same pattern: sex, interruption, crying, self-harm. The character makes no decisions. The situation resets at the end of each chapter to the same emotional baseline. The narrative is a loop, not an arc.
- **Correct execution:** Inciting incident → rising action → climax → resolution. Each chapter raises the stakes or changes the conditions. Remove the chapter breaks and the story still moves forward.

### Rule 7.2 — Describe Clothing Only When Relevant

Clothing descriptions must serve plot, character, or theme. They must not appear as recurring padding in every chapter.

- **Violation pattern:** A new outfit described in exhaustive detail in nearly every chapter. The same items recur: black leather, fishnets, combat boots, eyeliner, black lipstick. Descriptions never advance plot, character, or theme.
- **Correct execution:** Describe clothing when a character's appearance changes to signal a shift, when an outfit is relevant to a scene, or when it reveals something about the character. Most of the time, a single detail is sufficient.
